Are rising interest rates bad for the stock market?

Introduction

On January 10th, Federal Reserve Chairman Powell spoke at the hearing, saying that the economy after the epidemic may be different from the previous expansion, the US labor market is strong, and policies must be considered forward-looking, and tools will be used to support the economy and employment. market. In fact, since the Fed released the early signal of raising interest rates last week, the market’s expectations for interest rate hikes in March have begun to rise sharply.

Recently there has been a spike in concerns about the hawkish stance of Fed officials and the announced rise in Fed funds rates as a response to rising inflation. There have also been other corresponding events, like war in Ukraine, shortages of semiconductors, supply chain bottlenecks, energy shortages and other.

There has been a general concern about the coming bear market as a result of interest rates hikes. The dominant narrative around social media was that the rising interest rates will lead to recession and market slump.

The reasoning went as follows: as interest rates rise in the future, earnings of the companies that make up the index will most likely fall (since access to cheap capital going forward will slowly erode). If companies earn less and grow less, they are typically worth less. In discussing investments a lot of people seem to be “calling for” an upcoming and overdue global recession.

Principles of Economics by Professor Mankiw were often quoted: Because the Fed can influence interest rate and economic activity, it can alter the value of stocks. For example, when the Fed raises interest rates by reducing the money supply, it makes owning stocks less attractive for two reasons. First, a higher interest rate means that bonds, an alternative to stocks, earn a higher return. Second, a tightening of monetary policy reduces the demand for goods and services and thereby reduces profits. As a result, stock prices often fall when the Fed raises interest rate.

As of writing of this article traders are pricing in seven fed hikes this year.

But are those concerns justified? Is it really so that interest rate hikes contribute to sudden bear markets?

Objective

This is meant to be a very brief analysis, aiming to find out how historically interest rate hikes have influenced the S&P500 and Nasdaq stock indices.

Methodology

In order to compare how the Fed interest rate hikes influenced the stock market, data was collected on Fed funds rate from the official site of stlouisfed.org and index quotes for S&P500 and Nasdaq from stooq.com. A program was written using python and jupyter lab environment to collect the data and project it onto a graph. Following data collection, visual comparison of interest rate and stock indices graphs was performed.


Fig 1. Source code of the Fed funds rate analysis, made with Python and the Jupyter Lab environment Source: Own study

Results

The results are interesting although not that surprising.


Fig. 2. Selected time periods where interest rate hikes had a positive effect on the stock market. Source: Own study


The graph clearly shows that in most instances, whenever interest rate hike occurred, both the broad index represented by S&P500, and the technology index represented by Nasdaq, behaved quite strong. This is due to the fact that most interest rates hikes happen first when the economy is gaining momentum, therefore company revenues and profits tend to hold strong and so are the expectations.

Conclusion

Despite a clear sign that stocks tend to behave quite favorably during interest rate hikes, further studies have to be conducted to assess the scale of the stock market moves, as well as, circumstances in which they have occurred.
